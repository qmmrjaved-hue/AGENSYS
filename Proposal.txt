Workshop Introduction and Objectives
AGENSYS – Workshop on Knowledge Discovery, Maintenance and Distributed Intelligence in Multi-Agent Systems
The increasing adoption of Agentic AI systems is reshaping how complex tasks are addressed across a wide range of domains. In many emerging applications—such as collaborative information retrieval, distributed data analysis, simulation-based policy modeling, and automated scientific discovery—multiple agents interact, exchange intermediate outputs, and iteratively refine collective decisions over extended interaction cycles. At the same time, deploying these systems in practice requires distributed learning and coordination across heterogeneous sites, edge devices, and organizational boundaries, accelerating the transition toward intelligent, decentralized infrastructures in industrial and cyber-physical settings.
While multi-agent systems demonstrate remarkable capabilities, their widespread deployment introduces two deeply intertwined research challenges. On the one hand, fundamental questions arise concerning how knowledge is acquired, shared, validated, and updated through interaction—how learning unfolds over time, and how emergent patterns can be systematically analyzed. On the other hand, real-world environments rarely permit centralized training or global data sharing: learning must occur across distributed sites under privacy, regulatory, and communication constraints, demanding scalable and robust distributed training paradigms. At the intersection of these two dimensions, a central and still underexplored question emerges: how can multi-agent systems discover and remain grounded in reliable knowledge while learning and coordinating in a fully distributed manner?
This workshop jointly addresses knowledge-grounded multi-agent learning and distributed intelligence, treating them as complementary facets of the same overarching problem. Agents rely on external knowledge sources such as structured databases, knowledge graphs, retrieval systems, scientific corpora, or streaming data while collaborating or competing in dynamic environments. Simultaneously, the underlying training and adaptation processes must be decentralized, communication-efficient, and resilient to data heterogeneity. Within these settings, agents must integrate heterogeneous evidence, reconcile inconsistencies, adapt their strategies over time, and evolve collaboratively without centralized data aggregation.
The primary objective of AGENSYS is to provide a focused forum for investigating how knowledge discovery and distributed learning jointly shape the behavior, robustness, and scalability of multi-agent systems. To this end, the workshop brings together research communities spanning multi-agent learning, knowledge representation and reasoning, distributed and federated optimization, data mining in dynamic and networked environments, simulation-based learning, and explainable and trustworthy AI, fostering interdisciplinary dialogue on how knowledge can be effectively discovered, maintained, and validated in decentralized multi-agent architectures. We aim to address several concrete and currently underexplored challenges:
●	Grounding and coordination in distributed settings: How does access to heterogeneous external knowledge affect collaborative or competitive strategies among agents operating across distributed sites? How do retrieval-augmented agents coordinate when their retrieved evidence is partially inconsistent, and communication bandwidth is limited?
●	Hallucination propagation and distributed robustness: In iterative multi-agent pipelines, how can incorrect intermediate outputs spread and amplify across agents? What distributed mechanisms—such as cross-agent verification or decentralized consensus—can detect and contain error cascades without requiring a central authority?
●	Knowledge drift detection under decentralized learning: How can we formally detect when a multi-agent system progressively diverges from reliable knowledge sources during long-horizon interaction, especially when learning dynamics are governed by non-IID local data distributions?
●	Mitigation and resilience strategies: Can uncertainty modeling, confidence-weighted voting, graph-based consistency checks, or Byzantine-tolerant aggregation reduce collective errors in both the knowledge and distributed training dimensions?
●	Emergent knowledge generation: Under what conditions can distributed multi-agent interaction lead to the discovery of novel hypotheses or synthetic knowledge that is both valid and generalizable, going beyond what any single agent or centralized system could achieve?
●	Scalable distributed training for agentic architectures: How can we design communication-efficient, privacy-preserving, and resource-aware training pipelines that enable large-scale agentic systems to learn continuously across heterogeneous environments while maintaining knowledge consistency?
The added value for ECML-PKDD lies in providing a dedicated venue for in-depth discussion of agentic, interaction-based, and emergent learning paradigms at the nexus of knowledge discovery and distributed intelligence, complementing the main conference with contributions that explore how machine learning and data mining techniques can be extended to settings where knowledge is collectively constructed, dynamically evolving, and embedded in decentralized agent interactions. We encourage three forms of paper submissions: (i) full research papers, (ii) position papers, (iii) short papers. All submissions will be reviewed by an experienced multidisciplinary PC for their contributions in terms of novelty and relatedness to the workshop program. Diversity of the PC ensures the inclusion of representatives from related disciplines. Furthermore, we plan to organise a special issue in a journal, such as EPJ Data Science or ACM Transactions on Intelligent Systems and Technology, to feature selected contributions.

Call for Papers
Recent advances in agentic AI and multi-agent systems are reshaping how knowledge is discovered, represented, shared, and validated in dynamic, data-driven environments. In many emerging applications, agents interact—collaborating or competing—as adaptive learners that rely on external knowledge sources such as retrieval systems, knowledge graphs, structured databases, or streaming data. At the same time, training and deploying these systems at scale demands distributed learning paradigms that operate across heterogeneous sites, edge devices, and organizational boundaries under stringent privacy and communication constraints. The convergence of these two dimensions—knowledge-grounded reasoning and distributed intelligence—raises fundamental and still open questions: how can knowledge be reliably discovered, maintained, and validated when learning itself is decentralized?

The AGENSYS workshop provides a focused forum at the intersection of knowledge-grounded multi-agent learning and distributed intelligence. We welcome contributions that investigate how external knowledge shapes learning dynamics, coordination strategies, and robustness in multi-agent systems, as well as how distributed and decentralized training paradigms enable scalable, adaptive, and privacy-aware agentic architectures. We particularly encourage work that bridges these two perspectives, exploring how errors, uncertainty, or inconsistencies propagate across agents in decentralized settings.

We invite submissions on topics including, but not limited to:

Knowledge Discovery and Grounding in Multi-Agent Systems
●	Knowledge grounding and external knowledge integration in multi-agent systems
●	Learning and coordination under heterogeneous or inconsistent evidence
●	Hallucination propagation, detection, and containment in iterative agent pipelines
●	Knowledge drift detection and monitoring in long-horizon interaction
●	Uncertainty estimation and mitigation strategies in collaborative agents
●	Emergent knowledge generation and hypothesis discovery in agent collectives
●	Multi-agent reinforcement learning with knowledge constraints
●	Data mining over evolving interaction graphs

Distributed Intelligence and Decentralized Learning
●	Distributed and decentralized training of agentic architectures and foundation models
●	Decentralized optimization, consensus mechanisms, and convergence guarantees
●	Learning under heterogeneous, non-IID, and streaming data distributions
●	Continual and online adaptation in distributed and edge-constrained settings
●	Privacy-preserving and communication-efficient distributed learning
●	Cross-silo, cross-domain, and cross-device adaptation
●	Robustness, adversarial resilience, and Byzantine-tolerant aggregation

Evaluation, Trust, and Infrastructure
●	Evaluation frameworks and benchmarks for knowledge-grounded and distributed agent systems
●	Trust, explainability, and certifiability in distributed generative and agentic systems
●	Scalable infrastructure and benchmarking for distributed agentic AI

The workshop will accept three types of submissions: (i) full research papers, (ii) short papers, and (iii) position papers. All submissions must present original, unpublished work and clearly articulate their contribution to machine learning, data mining, or knowledge discovery in the context of knowledge-grounded and distributed multi-agent systems. We encourage methodological, theoretical, and empirical contributions that advance learning algorithms, evaluation frameworks, robustness analysis, or data mining techniques for interaction-driven, knowledge-constrained, and decentralized multi-agent environments.
The workshop is designed as a highly interactive, discussion-oriented event, combining formal presentations with collective debate. The tentative format includes oral presentations of accepted full papers. Depending on the number of accepted papers, sessions will be organized to maximize time for questions and feedback.
Logistics
The workshop requires a standard conference room equipped with a projector and basic presentation facilities. No additional logistical requirements are needed.

Proof of Interest from the Community
Research on agentic AI, multi-agent systems, and knowledge-grounded learning has experienced rapid growth in recent years, with increasing numbers of publications at major venues such as NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, AAAI, AAMAS, and The Web Conference. In particular, recent work on LLM-based agents, multi-agent reinforcement learning, and trustworthy AI highlights a strong and expanding interest in systems where knowledge is dynamically acquired, shared, and validated through interaction.

Several recent workshops have addressed related themes but none have specifically focused on knowledge-grounded multi-agent learning. This workshop aims to fill this gap by bringing together researchers working at the intersection of multi-agent learning, knowledge representation, dynamic data mining, robustness analysis, and evaluation of interaction-driven systems.

The topic naturally attracts researchers from multiple communities, including machine learning, data mining, multi-agent systems, natural language processing, network science, and trustworthy AI. Given the increasing number of projects and publications on LLM-based agents and multi-agent interaction, we expect strong interest from both academia and industry.

We plan to actively disseminate the Call for Papers through research networks and social media channels, and we are in contact with researchers working on knowledge-grounded and multi-agent learning systems who have expressed preliminary interest in participating in the workshop.
